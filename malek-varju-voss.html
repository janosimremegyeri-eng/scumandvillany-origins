Malek Voss

Ex-smuggler, information broker, and pragmatic leader.

Background

Malek flew in the service of the Hegemony for nearly twenty years. He was born and raised on Cadia, a defensive bastion of the Hegemony, where he completed the Academy. Over the years he was deployed across multiple sectors, fighting xeno forces, pirates, insurgents—and whatever else the war demanded. He genuinely believed the Hegemony kept the galaxy from sliding into chaos, and that would be far worse than the present order.

His career included fighters, interceptors, and bombers; for a time he served as second pilot on a larger corvette until injuries pulled him back into drydock and his crew was temporarily reassigned. He later served on a frigate, where he led a bomber wing for several years.

About five or six years ago his wing was ordered to suppress a planetary rebellion. Intelligence pinpointed insurgents hiding in a remote military outpost. As they closed the target they began picking up things that did not belong: civilian radio traffic, hospital frequencies, school beacons—there was no sign of military transmissions. Still, the order to bomb came through. Malek tried to buy time—he requested a review, new intel, or at least visual confirmation—but headquarters denied every request.

Acting on his own authority, Malek ordered his people not to fire. Half of his crews obeyed while the other half and the following flights did not. A horrific air battle erupted: Hegemony fighters firing on Hegemony fighters, soldiers and former comrades gunned down one another, and Malek’s wing was shredded in minutes. His ship was downed; he survived, more or less by chance. After recovering, he spent months in hiding, scraping by on black jobs. Then he heard that a surviving comrade was to be transferred from the hospital to a prison for execution. It was a trap: the comrade had died in the battle, and Malek was captured when he attempted a rescue. There could be no public trial—the command’s error could not be exposed—so he was quietly put away in a non‑existent prison in the PROCYON system.

He has been in the brig for roughly five years. He is resigned and stoic, avoids trouble, and his calm, restrained manner keeps him safe. Cellmates know little beyond his name and assume he was military. He spends his days reading, staring into space, and cataloguing details. He knows which guard takes how many seconds per patrol, which drone’s cooling fan is louder, where tools and pipes are hidden. He does not plan an escape—he only needs something to keep his mind occupied.

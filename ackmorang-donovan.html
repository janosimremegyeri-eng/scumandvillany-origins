Ackmorang Donovan

Former Starsmith technician turned freelance pilot and shipwright.

Background

Born on the busy orbital station called The Beginning of the End, on the Holt side of the Holt–Rin gate, Ackmorang grew up surrounded by every kind of establishment—lodging, entertainment, trade, workshops—and every kind of person, from imperial soldiers to pirate captains, humans to xenos. Many people fled through or to the station; scientists studying the gate’s closed side were not uncommon. Ackmorang is the son of Diana Hemra, a celebrated courtesan, and the roguish adventurer Javier Donovan. Javier had a short, intense affair with Diana and then, when he learned she was pregnant, suddenly had urgent business on the other side of the gate. Diana spent the rest of her life enchanted by him and treasured their son as the greatest gift of her love.

The child grew up loved, in modest circumstances; the station’s courtesans were protected because they were a major source of income. His mother sang endless praises of the wonderful Javier, and Ackmorang grew up hearing legends of his father as a hero or demi‑god.

From an early age the boy was a huge fan of old films and books—samurai, cowboy, and knight tales in particular. He idolized heroes who would face any danger for a righteous cause, even at the cost of their lives, and who, despite the empire’s power, remained respected and loved by ordinary people who would help them if they could. Those idealized images fit neatly with the marvelous stories he heard about his father, and he decided he wanted to be a beloved hero too.

Crime took many shapes and sizes on the station. Several syndicates would have gladly recruited Ackmorang; child thieves operated widely and successfully in the more dangerous districts. He never joined any gang, and whenever he or a friend was mugged or threatened he stood up to attackers. His first love was a girl named Linerva, who gave him a lasting preference for freckled, red‑haired women. His impression of the Imperials was that they were almost untouchable, but otherwise often no better than criminals—many were deeply entangled with the syndicates. Belonging to no one was both a blessing and a curse: he competed with no one but was also under no protection. Although the courtesans were respected to some degree, their children were not considered especially valuable.

In his early teens he first felt the Way and learned to use it instinctively to some degree. He was working as a bouncer at his mother’s workplace when Master Symerus, a member of the Guardians of the Void who was traveling in the system to study the closed gate, discovered him. The boy eagerly accepted the invitation; his mother was happy that her talented son could further hone his abilities. Rye, a romantic partner who could also attune, began training with him but proved unsuitable—the master did not sense a pure enough heart for guardianship—and Rye was sent home. The passionate affair ended quickly but peacefully; a friendship remained.

The Guardians of the Void interpret the Way in a distinctive way. They teach that these energies come from nothingness—symbols of black holes—and that if left unchecked they will consume the world. According to their doctrine, mystics who walk the Way must draw off and balance these accumulating energies. The sect possesses special Ur artifacts they call the Gates of the Void: fist‑sized, pitch‑black (absorbing all light), eight‑sided, metallic objects. There are eight of them in total (one per master). They are either gates to the same black hole or each contains its own miniature black hole (the former is more likely). Opening them requires special knowledge: the metal shell is sealed from the outside and contains eight locks that can only be opened telekinetically. Each lock opened increases the artifact’s power one level: the device siphons ambient energy and makes it available to its user (hence, even when closed, it passively drains a small amount of life and machine energy). Fully opened, only the most highly trained masters can control the flow, and the sect teaches that such control will be needed only at the Final Flood—the sect’s envisioned apocalypse—when balance is lost and the Guardians’ masters must restore the flow by taming nothingness. The locks’ locations and opening methods vary, but each lock is present in the same position on all eight devices. Every master knows one lock’s location and method. For safety, the full set of locations and combinations is not written down anywhere, but genuine and forged sect notes and weaker or clever copies of the gates regularly surface on the black market.

Ackmorang threw himself into spiritual study, pursuing the herohood he craved, though he was never great at concentration. His active childhood made him quick to master combat training. The sect recognizes three (or four) internal ranks: novice, teacher, and master. About halfway through becoming a novice, when instructors deem the candidate ready, a combat test is required; upon passing the test the initiate receives a chosen ritual melee weapon. Following his successful exam, Ackmorang, inspired by his heroes, requested a katana‑like sword made from a green, glass‑like material matching his eye color. These weapons can be charged with the user's learned energy (a psy‑blade mystical power). After passing the exam the initiate receives a first tattoo: the outline of a black circle on the left palm. This is effectively the zeroth rank—no official name, nicknamed “half” because it is halfway to the first rank. The true novice receives a full black circle tattoo; a teacher’s mark frames the circle with a square outline; a master’s mark adds another square outline (making an eight‑sided figure—the number eight is sacred to the sect). The novice ritual involves amplifying the candidate’s third eye under controlled conditions and, by opening one lock on an artifact, letting them “look into” the black hole so they see up close what they are fighting. The Empire had long hunted powerful artifacts, and somehow—perhaps because Blish or Rye were not present during the raid—the sect’s local cell (a ship) was found and struck down during that very ritual. Ackmorang’s rank tattoo therefore remained incomplete.

In the attack, the disturbed master forced open Ackmorang’s third eye rather than closing it properly; the boy plunged nearly his whole soul into the black hole, experiencing countless variants of non‑existence and total annihilation. This ordeal is the cause of his fur turning from black to snow‑white, his overwhelming fear of the dark, and his nightmares. The sect’s members there were slaughtered before the imperial mystic could question them; the helpless youth was the only captive. From there he was taken to prison, where they have tried ever since to extract the secret of opening the artifact from him—the secret Ackmorang, sadly, does not know.
